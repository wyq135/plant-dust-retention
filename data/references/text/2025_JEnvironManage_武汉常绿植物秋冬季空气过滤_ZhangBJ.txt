contrast to the TE concentration in winter, PM deposition on plants did not always increase after the autumn,
probably due to the park’s low density of vegetation, PM resuspension by wind, and a decrease in the plants’
physiological activity. Seasonal variations in pollutants accumulation among species were recorded during the
autumn/winter. This study reinforces the need for biodiversity and higher-density urban greening to optimize
post-growth air phytofiltration. A holistic, year-round air pollution mitigation strategy should be provided by
incorporating more diverse evergreen plant species with complementary phytofiltering properties.

are highest in the autumn and winter (Miao et al., 2022) when plant
phytoremediation activity is at its lowest (Przybysz et al., 2019; Motie
et al., 2023). Deciduous plants shed their leaves in autumn, while most
evergreen plants, especially coniferous ones, are sensitive to urban
conditions and high levels of pollution and cannot be planted too close
to PM sources (Gawron´ski et al., 2017; Reyes et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
2022a). Therefore, this study investigated the PM-accumulation capac-
ity of common species of leafy evergreen plants that are known for their
tolerance to urban conditions and are potential candidates for
post-growth air phytofiltration. All the plants examined accumulated
PM in the autumn and winter, but the intensity of this process varied
between the species. Shrubs and grass accumulated PM more efficiently
per leaf area unit than trees. This is consistent with Mo et al. (2015)and
Cai et al. (2017) results. Most often, however, comparing shrubs and
trees did not reveal differences in their ability to accumulate PM based
on growth form (Mo et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2018). The ability to accu-
mulate PM effectively is usually explained by the morphological char-
acteristics of the foliage (rough surface, hairs, thick wax coating) (Chiam
et al., 2019; Corada et al., 2021). However, the shrubs L. chinense,
P. tobira and R. simsii do not differ considerably in morphological
characteristics from the studied trees. In the context of PM accumulation
on the R. simsii, only a pronounced wax layer and a substantial pro-
portion of WPM are identified as primary determinants facilitating PM
adsorption. Apart from that, the leaves of examined shrubs are covered
with hairs, but their density is low. Enhanced PM deposition on shrubs
can be attributed to their proximity to the ground, which not only allows
direct PM interception from the air but also facilitates the accumulation
of PM that is either washed off from trees by precipitation or resus-
pended by the wind (Steinparzer et al., 2023). Moreover, the typically
compact and dense crown of shrubs enhances PM retention compared
with trees, but this is true in this work only for L. chinense (Steinparzer
et al., 2023).
The PM accumulation recorded in this study is in agreement with
typical values for deciduous plants as assessed during both summer and
winter (Chen et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Bui et al., 2022). This accu-
mulation surpasses that of senescent leaves retained on plants in winter
(Przybysz et al., 2019), yet remains substantially lower than that of
coniferous species (Przybysz et al., 2014, 2019; Jin et al., 2021; Zhang
et al., 2022). The distinct morphology features of conifers (brush-like
shoots, rough bark, needle sheaths, sticky secretions) enhance their
capacity to intercept and retain PM (Xu et al., 2018; Corada et al., 2021;
Steinparzer et al., 2023). However, excessive PM deposition on conifers
have negative effects on their physiology, and in extreme conditions
may culminate in plant mortality (Gawron´ski et al., 2017). Prolonged
exposure to escalating PM levels compromises coniferous species’
resilience to urban air pollution challenges (Burkhardt and Pariyar,
2014; Gawron´ski et al., 2017). In contrast, the species investigated in the
present study appeared to accumulate PM efficiently enough, suggesting
their potential inclusion in strategies to enhance winter air quality
without compromising their health. This is due to the simpler structure
of evergreen broadleaf plants, which not only accumulate less PM than
coniferous, but also have limited PM retention capacity, which is more
easily washed and/or blown away from foliage.

selection for air phytoremediation endeavours. In addition to high PM
deposition on the leaf, the plant should be characterised by sufficient LAI
(the one-sided green leaf area relative to the ground surface area). When
